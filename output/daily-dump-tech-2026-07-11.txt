Apple has filed a formal lawsuit against OpenAI in the Northern District of California, alleging a systematic campaign of trade secret theft. The complaint centers on a pattern of behavior by seven former Apple hardware engineers who migrated to OpenAI between November 2025 and March 2026. Apple claims these individuals transferred proprietary hardware data related to custom neural processing units and power management architectures. The filing includes specific allegations that these engineers bypassed company security protocols by using encrypted personal hardware devices to exfiltrate over four hundred gigabytes of confidential technical documentation, including schematics and test results, in the weeks before their resignations. Apple is seeking compensatory damages and a permanent injunction against the use of these specific design methodologies in OpenAI’s hardware product roadmap. The lawsuit also names IO Products as a co-defendant, alleging the entity was established as a shell company to facilitate the transfer of these digital assets and provide a layer of separation between the individuals and OpenAI. Apple’s internal audit logs, cited in the filing, indicate that access to these specific repository folders was restricted to a small team of twenty employees before the migration began.

In security news, a new ransomware strain is bypassing endpoint detection systems using a legitimate, Microsoft-signed kernel driver. The malware, identified by researchers as GodDamn ransomware, utilizes a driver known as PoisonX to terminate security software at the kernel level. Because the driver carries a valid Hardware Compatibility Publisher signature issued by Microsoft, it successfully evades traditional signature-based detection and heuristic monitoring tools that treat signed drivers as trusted system components. The attackers have already deployed this technique against at least ten enterprise hosts across the finance and logistics sectors. Once the driver is loaded and the security processes are disabled, the ransomware proceeds with full-disk encryption using an AES-256 algorithm. This exploit follows a similar pattern seen in previous incidents involving the exploitation of the Windows kernel driver signing ecosystem, where legitimate administrative tools, often designed for diagnostic purposes, are co-opted to provide high-level system access to malicious actors. Microsoft has since revoked the certificate associated with the PoisonX driver, rendering it untrusted for future installations.

Moving to the semiconductor market, SK Hynix has completed its initial public offering on the New York Stock Exchange, raising twenty-six billion dollars. This marks the largest foreign company debut in United States financial history, surpassing the twenty-five billion dollar record set by Alibaba in 2014. The capital injection comes at a time of record demand for high-bandwidth memory chips, known as HBM3E, which are essential for training large-scale artificial intelligence models. SK Hynix currently holds an estimated fifty-three percent market share in the high-bandwidth memory sector. The company is now facing direct pressure from United States regulators to commit to the construction of domestic fabrication facilities in Indiana to insulate the AI supply chain from geopolitical volatility. The stock opened at one hundred seventy dollars per share, signaling strong investor confidence in the long-term memory requirements of the current machine learning hardware cycle. This listing follows the company’s recent announcement of a four-billion-dollar investment in local packaging facilities.

In critical infrastructure news, Progress Software has issued an urgent directive for all ShareFile customers to immediately shut down Windows servers running its Storage Zone Controllers. The company confirmed it is responding to a credible external security threat that targets these specific deployments. As a precautionary measure, Progress has temporarily disabled access to all affected accounts to prevent further unauthorized activity. The vulnerability involves a flaw in the management interface that allows for potential remote exploitation of the underlying storage infrastructure. System administrators are advised to keep these controllers offline until the company releases a security patch, which is expected within the next forty-eight hours. The company has instructed users to verify the integrity of their data, as the exploit may allow attackers to download or replace files stored within the zone.

Finally, Meta has officially disabled a feature on Instagram that allowed users to generate artificial intelligence images based on content from public accounts via a simple tagging mechanism. The company removed the tool following widespread criticism regarding the unauthorized use of public data for model training and content synthesis. Meta stated that the feature failed to meet its internal standards for user control and consent, as it allowed content from any public profile to be referenced and manipulated by the AI generator without explicit user opt-in. The tool, which was released in a limited beta in early June, allowed users to input a tag that would then scrape a profile’s image history to generate new, synthetic variations. Meta has since issued a formal apology and confirmed that the underlying functionality has been purged from the platform, marking a pivot in how it approaches the integration of generative AI tools with user-generated content and privacy settings.
