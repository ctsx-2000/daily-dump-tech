It is July 15, 2026. This is your Daily Dump of tech news.

Google and Epic Games have jointly withdrawn their multi-year antitrust lawsuit regarding third-party app stores on the Android operating system. As part of this resolution, Google will permit rival app stores to operate natively within the Android ecosystem starting next week, removing the requirement for these stores to be sideloaded through browser downloads. This shift effectively dismantles the restrictive framework that previously forced third-party stores to navigate complex security warnings and permission prompts. The agreement also mandates that Google removes its automated system blocks that previously prevented third-party storefronts from updating their own applications without direct user intervention.

Microsoft has released its largest security update in the company's history, addressing at least five hundred and seventy unique security vulnerabilities. This rollout nearly triples the volume of fixes from the previous month, which totaled one hundred and ninety-two patches. The update covers critical flaws across the entire Windows eleven kernel, the Edge browser engine, and the underlying Azure cloud integration services. In a shift from previous policy, Windows eleven users now have the option to pause these updates indefinitely, a reversal of the prior mandatory update cadence that had been in place since the launch of the operating system.

In security, SonicWall is warning of zero-day exploits targeting its SMA1000 series hardware. Two specific vulnerabilities are currently being leveraged by threat actors to achieve unauthorized remote access and administrative control over enterprise gateways. The company has released emergency security patches for all affected units, which are primarily utilized by global corporations to manage remote access for employees. These vulnerabilities impact every firmware version released between January two thousand twenty-four and June two thousand twenty-six.

Regarding AI tool security, the SpaceXAI Grok coding assistant was found to be automatically uploading entire user codebases to cloud storage without explicit confirmation. The company disabled the feature after independent researchers identified that the tool was packaging local repositories into compressed archives during standard IDE operation. The telemetry logs confirmed that codebases exceeding several gigabytes were being transmitted to centralized servers, bypassing local-only configuration settings.

On the investment front, an OpenAI researcher is in late-stage discussions to launch an AI-focused drug discovery startup. The venture is currently seeing valuation estimates reach two billion dollars, signaling sustained capital interest in generative models applied to life sciences. This startup aims to utilize custom transformer architectures to predict molecular interactions, a process that historically required years of laboratory testing. The funding round is led by major venture firms that have previously backed autonomous robotics and high-frequency trading platforms.

Turning to labor issues, a new lawsuit alleges that Meta utilized artificial intelligence to manage mass layoff decisions during its recent workforce reduction. The filing claims the company terminated employees with disabilities and documented medical issues based on algorithmic output, an allegation Meta has denied. Plaintiffs in the class-action suit allege that the AI system targeted employees with high healthcare utilization rates and documented time-off requests. The legal complaint points to internal data logs that suggest the algorithm prioritized headcount reduction targets over individual performance metrics.

Spotify founder Daniel Ek is expanding his body-scanning startup, Neko Health, into the United States. Following a seven hundred million dollar funding round from high-profile investors, the company plans to launch its first diagnostic clinic in New York later this year as part of a rapid national expansion. The Neko diagnostic suite utilizes hundreds of sensors to perform a full-body scan in under ten minutes, capturing data on skin conditions, heart function, and blood markers. This expansion follows a two-year pilot program in Europe where the company processed over fifty thousand patient scans.

Finally, SpaceX has begun shipping its V5 residential Starlink dish in select markets. The new hardware features a smaller, lighter footprint than the V4 model and includes improved power efficiency, drawing roughly fifteen percent less electricity during peak operation. The V5 dish replaces the integrated motor-driven alignment system found in earlier versions with a fixed-pane design that utilizes advanced beamforming software to maintain satellite locks. Production is currently scaling to meet global demand, with shipments prioritizing regions with high density and limited cable infrastructure.
