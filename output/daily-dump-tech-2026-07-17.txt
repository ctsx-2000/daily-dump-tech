It is July 17, 2026. This is your Daily Dump of tech news.

Uber has entered into a definitive agreement to acquire Delivery Hero in an all-stock transaction valued at 14.8 billion dollars. This deal encompasses Delivery Hero’s operations across 70 countries, significantly expanding Uber Eats’ reach into markets in Asia and the Middle East where Delivery Hero currently maintains a market share exceeding 40 percent. The acquisition effectively merges two of the world's largest logistics networks, bringing Delivery Hero’s fleet of 400,000 independent couriers under the Uber corporate umbrella and positioning the company as the dominant food delivery platform operating outside of mainland China.

In AI software, Google has updated its Vids video creation platform to include personalized AI avatars. This integration replaces static presentation templates with synthetic presenters capable of lip-syncing text-to-speech outputs generated by the company’s latest language models. The update introduces Gemini Omni-powered tools that allow users to generate high-definition video assets from single-sentence text prompts or uploaded reference images. These tools automate the frame-by-frame editing process, reducing the time required to compile corporate training materials or internal communications by an average of 60 percent compared to the previous version of the software.

OpenAI has launched a 230-dollar hardware keyboard designed for integration with its agentic coding software. The peripheral features a dedicated input row mapped to specific model-based triggers and shortcut functions for automated code refactoring. This hardware launch occurs amidst an ongoing legal dispute between OpenAI and Apple. Apple’s complaint alleges that OpenAI misappropriated proprietary hardware architecture designs and haptic feedback patents during the development of this device, a claim OpenAI has formally denied in its recent court filings.

On the telecommunications front, T-Mobile has committed to restoring free lines that were removed during a recent forced plan migration. Over 150,000 subscriber accounts were affected by the migration, which automatically transitioned users from legacy unlimited plans to current premium service tiers. While the company is reinstating the promotional free-line credits that were stripped during the transition, the associated monthly price increases—which average 12 dollars per line—will remain in effect for all affected customers.

In security, a new macOS information-stealing malware called ClickLock has been identified. The malware utilizes a kernel-level exploit to terminate all active background processes, effectively freezing the user interface to force a system-level authentication prompt. When the user enters their administrator password to regain control of the machine, the malware harvests the credentials and transmits them to a remote command-and-control server. Security researchers report that the malware has successfully bypassed current versions of macOS Gatekeeper protections on over 10,000 identified systems.

Netflix disclosed in its second-quarter earnings report that approximately 300 titles on its platform have utilized generative AI. The company stated that the majority of these implementations occurred during post-production to improve visual output quality and rendering efficiency. This represents a 40 percent increase in AI-assisted content compared to the previous fiscal year, with the primary applications being background noise reduction, automated color grading, and the generative extension of peripheral scenery in digital backgrounds.

Valve has confirmed that it will continue to stock replacement batteries for the original liquid crystal display model of the Steam Deck. Despite the introduction of the OLED hardware variant, Valve and its partner iFixit are securing new inventory of the 40-watt-hour battery packs to ensure support for the over 3 million original Steam Deck units currently in circulation. This supply chain commitment ensures that replacement hardware will be available for purchase through at least the end of 2028.

Finally, SpaceX scrubbed its second launch attempt for the Starship version three rocket after several engines failed to ignite during the automated ignition sequence. Data telemetry confirmed that three of the six Raptor engines experienced a pressure anomaly during the startup cycle, triggering an automatic hold and abort command. The company has begun offloading propellant from the Super Heavy booster and has moved the vehicle back to the integration facility for diagnostic testing, with the next launch attempt now scheduled for the coming days.
